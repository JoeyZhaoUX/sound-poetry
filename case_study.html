02 — Overview

Four listening modes,
one experiment.

The starting point was a simple question in my project brief: what if you could see your own voice while reading a poem? SoundPoetry is my answer — a browser-based experiment built with Three.js and WebAudio that turns breath and voice into four visual languages.

Challenge
Every audio-reactive tool I tested felt like a DJ toy — rewarding rhythm, ignoring meaning. I wanted something that makes reading a poem aloud feel worthwhile, not just making noise at a screen.
My Role
Solo designer–developer. Responsible for research, concept development, motion design, audio analysis pipeline, front-end build, and this case study.
Toolchain
Three.js r128 · Web Audio API · GLSL shaders · EffectComposer (Bloom + FilmPass + Chromatic Aberration) · gif.js export · Figma for IA.
Outcomes
A 100 KB single-file HTML demo rendering 6,144 particles at 60 fps, 4 modes, 8 curated poems, and a downloadable GIF keepsake.
①
Sound is invisible. Poetry has always been an oral art form, but most digital poetry experiences strip out the voice entirely. I wanted to give spoken poetry a visual body — something you can watch and keep.

②

09 — Results

Craft, then feedback.

Tested with 9 peers over two weeks. Technical targets were met, but the real surprise was session length — people stayed 3–4× longer than on comparable tools, and kept coming back unprompted.

What Worked
Making "reading aloud" the core interaction kept every visual decision honest — if it didn't respond to voice, it didn't belong. The 8-poem curated list also helped — too many choices and people just freeze.
What I'd Change
Mode 4 (Resonance Field) confused people at first — 2 out of 9 testers thought it was broken during the first 5 seconds. Needs a clearer onboarding hint or a brief animation showing what to expect.
What I Learned
Putting everything in one HTML file turned out to be a huge advantage — anyone can open it in 2 seconds, no npm, no setup. For a portfolio piece, that accessibility matters a lot.
